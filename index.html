“Who knows the Nose?”

You might know that “The Nose” is the official unofficial nickname for the Illustrations team at Spectator, but do you truly know what makes it nose-worthy? This Easter season, we are divulging the details camouflaged in the most shadowy corners of our illustrators’ pieces that just make “scents.” In the spirit of being nosy—of learning more about the artistic visions and quirks of our team—we picked our illustrators’ brains on the vision and rationale behind the hidden details, images, and references, or “Easter eggs’’ in their illustrations. While these Easter eggs sometimes contain references to the article they accompany, they are also, more often than not, personal to the illustrator or connect to topics that are completely unrelated to Spec. The following selection contains just a few of the hidden details you may have missed within our illustrations. So put on your spec-tacles, and enjoy the hunt! Who “nose” what you might sniff out next?

Illustration by Yingjie Wang

Illustration by Yingjie Wang

Yingjie: In the imagining of a modern New York City intellectual salon for the profile article about the Morningside Institute, stained glass windows and floor-to-ceiling bookshelves are quintessential furnishings. Amid the conversation and exchange, a lamp illuminates the space at the center of the illustration. Perched above the two lights, the owl gazing down is a reference to the owl logo of the Morningside Institute, tying the space together with the work and goals of the Institute.

Illustration by Carlos Sanchez-Tata

Carlos: For this illustration, the hearts and color palette were inspired by Daya Betty’s “Heart On” runway look from season 14 of “Rupaul’s Drag Race.” I wanted the drawing to feel child-like, almost as if a teenage girl drew it. With that in mind, I also took inspiration from the kitschy designs of Lisa Frank, who draws a lot of cute animals and hearts. These inspirations mainly influenced the color choices and rays of hearts for this Datamatch illustration.

Illustration by Noelle Hunter

Illustration by Noelle Hunter

Noelle: One of the contributing factors to the 1968 protests on campus was the proposed construction of a new University gym in Morningside Park, which was nicknamed “Gym Crow” due to its segregation of entrances—the main door served Columbia students, while the “back door” served Harlem residents. For this historical piece, I decided to create a watercolor interpretation of the design. The pathway leading up to the door represents the segregated “back door” entrance, and the stark contrast in facade colors indicates the extent of gym services that were available to Harlem residents—only a small portion of the bottom floor.

‘Gym Crow’: Looking back on the 1968 Morningside Gym protests

Illustration by Sadia Sharif

Illustration by Julian Michaud

Julian: I used practice exams from my economics, biology, and electrical engineering classes to create a paper-textured background for an article about midterms and stress culture. Illustration by Chiara Guigou

Illustration by Chiara Guigou

Chiara: Kofi’s experience as a DJ in New York City was particularly shaped by Brooklyn and, more specifically, by his role at Half Moon BK. To reference Kofi’s work as a radio host for the company, I wanted to use Brooklyn in the representation of New York City in the illustration. To that end, the skyline on the record at the bottom left of the piece is a view of Manhattan from Brooklyn.
